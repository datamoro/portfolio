Public Health Exploratory Analysis

Analysis of 458k+ Records

Context & Motivation

I’ve always believed that data becomes meaningful only when it helps us understand people and the systems that shape their realities. As part of my portfolio development, I decided to analyze the 2024 Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) with a specific focus on inequality in access to care and chronic disease burden across the United States.

My objective was to take a nationally representative dataset and translate it into a coherent story about how socioeconomic conditions impact health, bringing together data engineering, statistical reasoning, and public health sensibility.

Key Findings

Access & Barriers to Care

When breaking the data down by income, a clear gradient emerges: lower-income adults are significantly more likely to delay care due to cost, have more physically and mentally unhealthy days, and are less likely to receive routine checkups.

The geographic patterns reinforce this: states with persistent poverty tend to show higher cost-related barriers, highlighting how policy environments and structural conditions shape everyday health decisions.

Chronic Disease Inequality

I found substantial variation across sex, education, and employment status. Adults who are “unable to work” display some of the highest burdens across multiple chronic conditions, particularly depression and COPD.

When aggregating nine chronic conditions, nearly one in three adults presents two or more comorbidities, and this burden concentrates among those with fewer socioeconomic advantages.

Reflection

This project reflects the direction I want to grow in: combining technical skills with a deeper understanding of how social position shapes health outcomes.
